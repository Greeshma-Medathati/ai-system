ABSTRACT
This literature review examines two distinct but critical areas concerning Large Language Models (LLMs): performance optimization and intrinsic safety vulnerabilities. One study introduces EdgeShard, a novel approach for efficient distributed LLM inference across collaborative edge devices and cloud servers. EdgeShard employs dynamic programming to optimize inference latency and throughput by adaptively determining LLM partitioning and deployment strategies under varying network conditions. Experiments show it reduces memory usage, enables larger batch sizes, and outperforms cloud-edge methods, especially with insufficient cloud bandwidth. Conversely, another paper investigates conceptual failure modes of LLM safety training, specifically "competing objectives" and "mismatched generalization." These hypotheses informed the design of effective jailbreak attacks that successfully bypassed safety mechanisms in advanced models like GPT-4 and Claude v1.3, outperforming existing ad hoc methods. This highlights the dual challenge of optimizing LLM deployment efficiency while simultaneously addressing fundamental vulnerabilities to ensure robust safety-capability parity.

METHODS COMPARISON
While both papers address aspects of Large Language Models (LLMs), they employ distinctly different methodologies and technical foci. One paper (007e7d6cb64a7dfcdcad9fc6f4f0ba69fdc88203.pdf) proposes EdgeShard, a system that utilizes dynamic programming algorithms to solve a joint device selection and model partition problem, aiming to optimize LLM inference performance, latency, and throughput across collaborative edge devices and cloud servers. Its system design centers on efficient distributed deployment and resource management under various heterogeneous network conditions. In stark contrast, the other paper (929305892d4ddae575a0fc23227a8139f7681632.pdf) focuses on hypothesizing conceptual failure modes of LLM safety training—specifically competing objectives and mismatched generalization—to guide the design of effective jailbreak attacks. This approach frames the problem as an analysis of inherent safety vulnerabilities and emphasizes the need for safety-capability parity, reflecting a technical focus on adversarial robustness and ethical AI rather than operational performance.

RESULTS SYNTHESIS
While both papers operate within the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), and safety is identified as a common domain-specific theme, their primary contributions diverge. One study focuses on optimizing LLM inference performance, proposing EdgeShard and edgesplit for adaptive partitioning and deployment across heterogeneous networks. This method was shown to improve inference latency and throughput, reduce memory usage, and enable larger batch sizes, notably outperforming cloud-edge collaborative inference under insufficient cloud bandwidth. In contrast, the other paper investigates LLM safety mechanisms, hypothesizing "competing objectives" and "mismatched generalization" as conceptual failure modes in safety training. New attacks crafted from these principles were highly effective, succeeding on every prompt in red-teaming evaluation sets and surpassing existing ad hoc jailbreaks against state-of-the-art models, thereby emphasizing the necessity for safety-capability parity over reliance on scaling alone.
