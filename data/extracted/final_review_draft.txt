ABSTRACT
This literature review examines key aspects of Artificial Intelligence (AI) development, spanning safety, explainability, and broader societal impacts. One study demonstrates methods for training harmless AI assistants through self-improvement, showing that harmlessness and reliability improve with revisions, leveraging reinforcement learning and chain-of-thought for transparency. Another paper proposes taxonomies for Explainable AI (XAI), identifying explainability as a crucial barrier for widespread deployment and integral to Responsible AI. A third perspective highlights AI's potential for societal benefits and economic development, yet cautions against negative impacts on vulnerable groups and stresses the importance of AI augmenting human work and effective human-AI interaction. Collectively, these papers showcase varied approaches: one emphasizes empirical safety methods, another focuses on methodological advancements for explainability, and the third explores broader societal results and challenges, illustrating diverse perspectives on responsible AI deployment.

METHODS COMPARISON
The papers reviewed exhibit distinct technical foci and problem framings regarding artificial intelligence. "Constitutional AI" details an experimental methodology for training a harmless AI assistant through self-improvement, leveraging Supervised Learning (SL) and Reinforcement Learning (RL) stages with chain-of-thought reasoning to improve performance and transparency. This approach is highly focused on a specific system design for AI safety, measuring improvements in "harmlessness" and "HH scores" through an iterative revision process without requiring human labels for harmful outputs. In contrast, "Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI)" takes a more conceptual approach, proposing taxonomies for explaining various Machine Learning models, including Deep Learning, and framing explainability as a critical component for "Responsible Artificial Intelligence" and its large-scale implementation. This work identifies the "barrier of explainability" as an inherent problem in modern sub-symbolic AI techniques. Meanwhile, "Artificial Intelligence (AI): Multidisciplinary perspectives" adopts a broader scope, examining the societal challenges, opportunities, and policy implications of AI, emphasizing strategic human-AI interaction and the need for AI to augment rather than replace human work, thereby framing the problem within organizational and policy strategy rather than specific technical AI development or model interpretability.

RESULTS SYNTHESIS
One paper demonstrates that AI self-improvement methods can enhance harmlessness and human-judged performance, with the reinforcement learning (RL) stage significantly improving reliability, although helpfulness scores may decrease with revisions. In contrast, another study identifies explainability as a critical barrier for the practical deployment of modern AI models, particularly deep learning, emphasizing the need for taxonomies and a "Responsible AI" methodology centered on fairness, explainability, and accountability. Complementarily, a third perspective underscores AI's potential to improve quality of life and climate understanding but cautions about negative impacts on vulnerable societies and the critical need for effective human-AI interaction strategies that augment rather than replace human work. These findings highlight a divergence in focus, with some research providing technical results on AI safety and transparency, while others predominantly address the conceptual frameworks, challenges, and broader societal implications necessary for responsible and effective AI integration and deployment.
