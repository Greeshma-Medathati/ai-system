ABSTRACT
This review examines two distinct yet complementary facets of responsible AI development: AI harmlessness and explainable artificial intelligence (XAI). Constitutional AI research demonstrates that Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) significantly improves AI models' ability to follow instructions and achieve greater harmlessness, with specific probability clamping strategies further optimizing results. However, robustness against adversarial attacks remains a key challenge. Concurrently, XAI research highlights how integrating background knowledge enhances both explainability and performance. It introduces algorithms for bias reduction, exemplified in recidivism prediction, and underscores the intricate relationship between data fusion, privacy, and fairness. While Constitutional AI mainly focuses on results, method, and safety, XAI primarily centers on method, results, and evaluation. Despite lacking strong domain-specific common themes, these papers collectively illuminate the diverse methodological and evaluative considerations essential for ethical AI.

METHODS COMPARISON
The two papers adopt distinct methodological approaches and problem framings within the broader field of AI. "Constitutional AI" (CAI) primarily focuses on developing and refining methods for training AI models to exhibit specific behavioral properties, specifically harmlessness and robustness against "red-team attacks," by employing techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and fine-tuning model probability distributions. Its system design is centered on creating models that inherently prefer "thoughtfully harmless" responses. In contrast, "Explainable Artificial Intelligence" (XAI) presents a broader, more conceptual review of methods for understanding, evaluating, and mitigating issues in AI systems to promote "Responsible AI." This paper explores diverse technical foci, including the integration of background knowledge from Knowledge Bases for improved explainability, algorithms for bias reduction demonstrated in use cases like recidivism prediction, frameworks for reconstructing visual information from CNN internal representations, and the definition of new metrics for explainability. While CAI concentrates on engineering a specific attribute into an AI system, XAI provides a taxonomic overview of tools and challenges for interpreting and ensuring fairness, privacy, and accountability across a variety of AI applications.

RESULTS SYNTHESIS
The analyzed papers offer distinct but complementary insights into AI system development, primarily focusing on methodological advancements and empirical outcomes without strong domain-specific common themes. *Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback* demonstrates that Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) significantly improves models' instruction following and harmlessness, with the 'HH model' proving notably more harmless than helpful variants. This work found that specific interventions, such as clamping probabilities at 40-60% and instructing crowdworkers to prioritize thoughtful harmlessness, further enhanced results, though robustness against red-team attacks remains a key concern. In contrast, *Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI)* highlights methods for improving explainability and reducing bias; it establishes that integrating background knowledge not only boosts explainability but also enhances performance compared to purely data-driven approaches. This paper also proposes an algorithm for explanation generation and bias reduction, illustrated in recidivism prediction, and observes the intrinsic connection between data fusion, privacy, and fairness, alongside frameworks for reconstructing visual information from CNN internal representations. Collectively, these studies underscore varied technical pathways to advance responsible AI, addressing challenges from model safety and instruction adherence to transparency and fairness.
