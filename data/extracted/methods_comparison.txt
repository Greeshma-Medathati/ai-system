While both papers address aspects of Large Language Models (LLMs), they employ distinctly different methodologies and technical foci. One paper (007e7d6cb64a7dfcdcad9fc6f4f0ba69fdc88203.pdf) proposes EdgeShard, a system that utilizes dynamic programming algorithms to solve a joint device selection and model partition problem, aiming to optimize LLM inference performance, latency, and throughput across collaborative edge devices and cloud servers. Its system design centers on efficient distributed deployment and resource management under various heterogeneous network conditions. In stark contrast, the other paper (929305892d4ddae575a0fc23227a8139f7681632.pdf) focuses on hypothesizing conceptual failure modes of LLM safety training—specifically competing objectives and mismatched generalization—to guide the design of effective jailbreak attacks. This approach frames the problem as an analysis of inherent safety vulnerabilities and emphasizes the need for safety-capability parity, reflecting a technical focus on adversarial robustness and ethical AI rather than operational performance.
