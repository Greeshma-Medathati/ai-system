The two papers adopt distinct methodological approaches and problem framings within the broader field of AI. "Constitutional AI" (CAI) primarily focuses on developing and refining methods for training AI models to exhibit specific behavioral properties, specifically harmlessness and robustness against "red-team attacks," by employing techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and fine-tuning model probability distributions. Its system design is centered on creating models that inherently prefer "thoughtfully harmless" responses. In contrast, "Explainable Artificial Intelligence" (XAI) presents a broader, more conceptual review of methods for understanding, evaluating, and mitigating issues in AI systems to promote "Responsible AI." This paper explores diverse technical foci, including the integration of background knowledge from Knowledge Bases for improved explainability, algorithms for bias reduction demonstrated in use cases like recidivism prediction, frameworks for reconstructing visual information from CNN internal representations, and the definition of new metrics for explainability. While CAI concentrates on engineering a specific attribute into an AI system, XAI provides a taxonomic overview of tools and challenges for interpreting and ensuring fairness, privacy, and accountability across a variety of AI applications.
