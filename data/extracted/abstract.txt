This literature review examines two distinct but critical areas concerning Large Language Models (LLMs): performance optimization and intrinsic safety vulnerabilities. One study introduces EdgeShard, a novel approach for efficient distributed LLM inference across collaborative edge devices and cloud servers. EdgeShard employs dynamic programming to optimize inference latency and throughput by adaptively determining LLM partitioning and deployment strategies under varying network conditions. Experiments show it reduces memory usage, enables larger batch sizes, and outperforms cloud-edge methods, especially with insufficient cloud bandwidth. Conversely, another paper investigates conceptual failure modes of LLM safety training, specifically "competing objectives" and "mismatched generalization." These hypotheses informed the design of effective jailbreak attacks that successfully bypassed safety mechanisms in advanced models like GPT-4 and Claude v1.3, outperforming existing ad hoc methods. This highlights the dual challenge of optimizing LLM deployment efficiency while simultaneously addressing fundamental vulnerabilities to ensure robust safety-capability parity.
