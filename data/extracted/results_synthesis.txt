While both papers operate within the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), and safety is identified as a common domain-specific theme, their primary contributions diverge. One study focuses on optimizing LLM inference performance, proposing EdgeShard and edgesplit for adaptive partitioning and deployment across heterogeneous networks. This method was shown to improve inference latency and throughput, reduce memory usage, and enable larger batch sizes, notably outperforming cloud-edge collaborative inference under insufficient cloud bandwidth. In contrast, the other paper investigates LLM safety mechanisms, hypothesizing "competing objectives" and "mismatched generalization" as conceptual failure modes in safety training. New attacks crafted from these principles were highly effective, succeeding on every prompt in red-teaming evaluation sets and surpassing existing ad hoc jailbreaks against state-of-the-art models, thereby emphasizing the necessity for safety-capability parity over reliance on scaling alone.
